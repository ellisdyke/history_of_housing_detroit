History of Housing and Racism in Detroit

For over a century, housing in Detroit has reflected deeply entrenched racial and economic divides. This project explores how discriminatory policies like redlining, urban renewal, and restrictive covenants shaped who was allowed to live where — and how those patterns persist today. Through interactive data visualizations, we track changes in land use and property ownership that mirror larger patterns of segregation and inequity.

Our explorable visualizer focuses on properties sold through the Detroit Land Bank Authority (DLBA) from 2014–2024. We show how many of these properties remain unoccupied, while others achieve re-occupancy through Certificates of Occupancy. The animated map below helps illustrate both progress and ongoing challenges in reversing decades of disinvestment.

Explore the Data

Use the animation slider or “Play” button to scroll through property activity year by year. Gray dots show properties still owned by DLBA, orange dots represent DLBA sales, and green dots mark parcels that became re-occupied. Together, these layers visualize the slow rebuilding of Detroit’s neighborhoods — and the gaps that remain.
